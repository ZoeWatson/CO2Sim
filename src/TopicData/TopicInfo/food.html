Background:

Food production and distribution is estimated to be roughly one third of total yearly CO2 emissions (Buzby). Some of the key contributions to this are; land changes for agriculture such as slash and burn and deforestation releasing stored CO2, livestock production and fertilisers directly emitting methane and nitrous oxide, food waste, and the energy lost to the transportation, processing, and packaging of food (Buzby, Canada).

Personally, we can help address this by voting with our dollar, buying local, vegan, low packaging foods and avoiding foods that are produced in CO2e intensive ways. It’s important to remember, however, voting with your dollar only works when you have a dollar to spend. As a citizen you can encourage your local and national government to support subsidies for low CO2e foods and adding tariffs or banning food that are produced using unsustainable practices. You can also support vertical farms, and other city farms that allow food to be grown closer to where it's consumed. Thus cutting down on transportation and packing. Additionally, you can support programs that help get food that would be otherwise wasted to people in need.

Variables:

Each food category has a range from 0-200% of the current global production. You can drag the slider to see what would happen if we decrease, or increase the world’s production of that food.

The global Co2e for beef  was taken directly from Statista (Global animal).

The rest of the values were taken from Our World in Data, where they were recorded by CO2e per kg.  I then found the global production in kg of each food source and multiplied it by co2e per kg.

Limitations:
This model does not take into account calories per CO2 tonnes emitted. For example the global co2e for rice production is 2.26 gigatonnes per year, where eggs are 0.28 gigatonnes per year. Looking at it this way seems like eggs are way better for the planet than rice! However, if you consider co2e per kg of food the numbers look very different. Eggs produce 4.7 kg of CO2 per kg, whereas rice only produces 4.5 kg (Food). Furthermore this doesn't even consider the nutritional density of each type of food. A kg of olive oil or chocolate is a lot more nutritionally dense than a kg of salad!

Additionally the co2e for food production relies heavily on energy and transportation. Changes in CO2e for those sectors would affect the CO2e in the food sector in real life, but for this sim I didn’t have the data available to model that correctly.
